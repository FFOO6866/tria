## 2. Communication Principles

### 2.1 Core Principles

#### 1. Clarity First
- Use simple, direct language
- Avoid ambiguity
- Structure information logically
- Confirm understanding

#### 2. Customer-Centric
- Address customer's specific needs
- Use "you" more than "we"
- Focus on benefits to customer
- Acknowledge their situation

#### 3. Responsive
- Reply promptly
- Acknowledge receipt of messages
- Set clear expectations
- Follow up proactively

#### 4. Consistent
- Maintain same voice across channels
- Align with brand guidelines
- Use standard terminology
- Deliver reliable experiences

#### 5. Respectful
- Honor customer's time
- Respect business relationship
- Maintain professional boundaries
- Show cultural awareness

---

### 2.2 Communication Goals

Every customer interaction should aim to:

1. **Resolve** - Address the customer's need or question completely
2. **Reassure** - Build confidence in TRIA's capability and reliability
3. **Educate** - Help customer make informed decisions
4. **Strengthen** - Deepen the business relationship
5. **Delight** - Exceed expectations where possible

## 4. Language Standards

### 4.1 Words to Use Frequently

**Positive & Action-Oriented:**
- Absolutely
- Certainly
- Happy to help
- I'll take care of that
- No problem
- Perfect
- Right away
- Thank you

**Clear & Specific:**
- Confirm
- Ensure
- Exactly
- Specifically
- Your order includes...
- This means...
- Here's what happens next...

**Customer-Focused:**
- You'll receive...
- For your business...
- Your delivery...
- Your needs...
- How can I help...

---

### 4.2 Words to Avoid

**Negative/Limiting:**
- Can't (use "unable to" or offer alternative)
- Won't
- Unfortunately (use sparingly)
- Impossible
- Never
- Problem (use "issue" or "situation")

**Vague/Uncertain:**
- Maybe
- Might
- Possibly
- Should
- I think
- I'll try

**Overly Technical:**
- B-flute corrugation (say "durable cardboard")
- Polypropylene (say "food-grade plastic")
- ETA (say "expected delivery time")
- SKU (say "product code" or just use description)

**Overly Casual:**
- Yup/Nope
- Hey
- Guys
- Cool
- Awesome (acceptable occasionally, but not default)

---

### 4.3 Sentence Structure

**Keep It Clear:**
- Short sentences (15-20 words ideal)
- One idea per sentence
- Active voice preferred
- Bullet points for lists

**Good Example:**
"Your order is confirmed. Here's what you'll receive:
- 600 boxes (10")
- 200 boxes (12")
- 1,600 liners (free)
Total: SGD 1,080 including GST
Delivery: Thursday, Oct 20, 2-5 PM"

**Poor Example:**
"We have processed your order which consists of six hundred units of the ten-inch pizza boxes and two hundred units of the twelve-inch pizza boxes, and please note that the single facer liners will be provided complimentary based on the total quantity of boxes ordered, and your invoice total including the eight percent goods and services tax amounts to one thousand and eighty Singapore dollars, with delivery scheduled for Thursday."

---

### 4.4 Singaporean English (Singlish) Usage

**General Guideline:**
- Professional written communication: Standard English
- WhatsApp with established customers: Light Singlish acceptable
- Never use Singlish that could confuse or appear unprofessional

**Acceptable (in context):**
- "Can!" (when confirming capability)
- "No problem lah" (casual reassurance to familiar customers)
- "Okay, noted!" (acknowledgment)
- "Confirm plus chop" (very informal - only with very familiar customers)

**Not Acceptable:**
- Grammatically incorrect constructions
- Overly colloquial expressions
- Anything that obscures meaning
- Use with international customers

**When in Doubt:** Use Standard English

## 6. Scenario-Based Communication

### 6.1 Order Confirmations

**Tone:** Efficient, positive, clear
**Key Elements:** Acknowledge receipt, confirm details, set expectations
**Length:** Brief (3-5 lines)

**Example:**
"Order confirmed! ‚úÖ

üì¶ Items:

- 200 x 12" boxes

üí∞ Total: SGD 1,080 (incl. GST)
üöö Delivery: Thursday, Oct 20, 2-5 PM

---

### 6.2 Answering Product Questions

**Tone:** Helpful, informative, consultative
**Key Elements:** Direct answer first, then details, then offer to help further
**Length:** Medium (5-8 lines)

**Example:**
"Great question! Our 14-inch boxes are SGD 0.85 each (plus 8% GST).

They're sold in bundles of 50 pieces, so:
- 1 bundle (50 pcs) = SGD 45.90 including GST
- 400 pieces (8 bundles) = SGD 367.20 including GST

---

### 6.3 Handling Complaints

**Tone:** Empathetic, apologetic, solution-focused
**Key Elements:** Acknowledge, apologize, explain (if relevant), solve, follow up
**Length:** Medium to long (7-10 lines)

**Example:**
"I'm sorry to hear about this issue, Marcus. You're absolutely right - this isn't the quality we promise.

Here's what I'm doing right away:
1. Sending replacement boxes (50 pcs) tomorrow morning, no charge
2. Flagging this with our quality team for investigation
3. Adding a SGD 50 credit to your account as an apology

Your replacement will arrive between 9-11 AM tomorrow. I'll personally confirm with you once it's dispatched.

Thank you for bringing this to our attention. We really value your business and want to make this right."

---

### 6.4 Delivering Bad News

**Tone:** Honest, regretful, helpful
**Key Elements:** Direct but kind truth, reason (if appropriate), alternatives, show care
**Length:** Medium (6-8 lines)

**Example:**
"I understand you need delivery tomorrow, and I wish I could make that work. Unfortunately, our next available delivery slot is Thursday.

Here are your options:
1. Thursday morning (9-12 PM) - standard delivery
2. Wednesday delivery to nearby area, you arrange pickup - no delivery charge
3. I can check with our express courier partner - estimated additional cost SGD 80

Which would work best for you? I want to help find a solution that keeps your business running smoothly."

---

### 6.5 Upselling/Cross-Selling

**Tone:** Helpful, not pushy, value-focused
**Key Elements:** Relevant suggestion, benefit to customer, easy to say no
**Length:** Short to medium (4-6 lines)

**Example:**
"Your order is confirmed!

Quick thought: I noticed you ordered 200 meal trays. Would you like to add lids? They're SGD 0.15 each and keep food fresh during delivery. Most of our pizza outlets find them essential.

Let me know if you'd like me to add them - happy to help either way!"

**Poor Example (Too Pushy):**
"You really should order lids with those trays. All our customers buy them. You'll regret it if you don't. They're only SGD 0.15 each which is a great price. Let me add 200 to your order now."

---

### 6.6 Following Up

**Tone:** Attentive, helpful, not nagging
**Key Elements:** Reference previous interaction, offer value, easy out
**Length:** Short (3-4 lines)

**Example:**
"Hi Jennifer! üëã

## 7. Cultural Sensitivity (Singapore Context)

### 7.1 Multicultural Awareness

Singapore is multicultural with Chinese, Malay, Indian, and other communities. Our communication should:

**Be Inclusive:**
- Don't assume customer's ethnicity, religion, or background
- Use neutral greetings ("Hi" vs. culturally specific greetings)
- Avoid cultural stereotypes
- Respect all dietary/religious practices

**Recognize Diversity:**
- Names: Use customer's stated name/spelling
- Holidays: Acknowledge major festivals of all communities
- Language: Accept English, Singlish, and occasional other languages gracefully
- Customs: Be aware of major religious observances (Ramadan, Chinese New Year, Deepavali, etc.)

---

### 7.2 Festive Season Greetings

**Inclusive Approach:**
"Wishing you a joyful festive season and prosperous year ahead!"

**Specific Festivals (if you know customer celebrates):**
- "Happy Chinese New Year! üßß"
- "Selamat Hari Raya! üåô"
- "Happy Deepavali! ü™î"
- "Merry Christmas! üéÑ"
- "Happy Holidays!"

**When in Doubt:** Use generic "Happy Holidays" or "Season's Greetings"

---

### 7.3 Business Culture Norms

**Hierarchy & Respect:**
- Address people with appropriate titles (Mr., Mrs., Ms., Dr.)
- Show respect for seniority
- Use formal tone until customer initiates casual tone

**Time Consciousness:**
- Singaporeans value punctuality
- Always provide specific timeframes
- Apologize for delays, even small ones
- Respect business hours

**Efficiency:**
- Get to the point relatively quickly
- Provide clear, actionable information
- Don't waste time with excessive small talk (in business context)
- Value practical solutions over theory

**Face & Harmony:**
- Avoid public criticism or blame
- Handle mistakes with grace and discretion
- Offer solutions that allow customer to save face
- De-escalate conflicts diplomatically

---

### 7.4 Language Considerations

**Singapore English:**
- Accept various English proficiency levels
- Clarify politely if you don't understand
- Use simple English with customers who may not be native speakers
- Be patient with typos/grammar (focus on intent)

**Code-Switching:**
- Customer may switch between formal and casual English
- Match customer's formality level
- Don't correct customer's English
- Ensure your meaning is clear regardless of style

## 9. Do's and Don'ts

### 9.1 Communication Do's

‚úÖ **Always:**
- Greet the customer by name (if known)
- Acknowledge receipt of their message
- Provide specific information (dates, times, amounts)
- Offer clear next steps
- Thank customers for their business
- Proofread before sending
- Follow up on promises
- Take ownership ("I'll take care of this")
- Use active voice ("We'll deliver Thursday" vs. "Your order will be delivered")
- Show appreciation for their patience/understanding

---

### 9.2 Communication Don'ts

‚ùå **Never:**
- Ignore a message (even if you can't solve immediately, acknowledge)
- Use ALL CAPS (except acronyms like GST, SGD)
- Blame the customer or other departments
- Make promises you can't keep
- Use sarcasm or humor that could be misunderstood
- Share confidential information
- Discuss other customers
- Show frustration or annoyance
- Use technical jargon without explanation
- End conversation without clear resolution or next step

---

### 9.3 Tone Pitfalls to Avoid

**Too Robotic:**
‚ùå "Your request has been received and is being processed according to standard operating procedures."
‚úÖ "Got it! I'm processing your order now and will confirm shortly."

**Too Casual:**
‚ùå "Hey! Yup, no prob, we got u covered lol üëçüëçüëç"
‚úÖ "Hi! Yes, we can definitely help with that. üëç"

**Too Wordy:**
‚ùå "I wanted to take this opportunity to reach out to you in order to inform you that we have unfortunately encountered a situation..."
‚úÖ "I need to update you on a delivery delay..."

**Too Apologetic:**
‚ùå "I'm so terribly sorry to bother you, I apologize profusely, please forgive me for taking up your valuable time..."

**Defensive:**

## 11. Voice & Tone Checklist

Before sending any customer communication, ask yourself:

**Tone Check:**
- [ ] Is my tone appropriate for the situation?
- [ ] Am I being respectful and professional?
- [ ] Does it sound human, not robotic?
- [ ] Is it warm but not overly casual?

**Clarity Check:**
- [ ] Is my message clear and easy to understand?
- [ ] Have I provided specific information (dates, amounts, etc.)?
- [ ] Are there clear next steps?
- [ ] Could a non-expert understand this?

**Customer-Centric Check:**
- [ ] Am I addressing the customer's specific need?
- [ ] Am I offering solutions, not just problems?
- [ ] Have I used "you" more than "we"?
- [ ] Would I be satisfied with this response if I were the customer?

**Brand Alignment Check:**
- [ ] Does this sound like TRIA (professional yet approachable)?
- [ ] Am I being proactive and helpful?
- [ ] Have I maintained consistency with our brand voice?
- [ ] Would this make the customer feel valued?

**Quality Check:**
- [ ] Are there spelling or grammar errors?
- [ ] Is the formatting clean and readable?
- [ ] Are emojis used appropriately (if at all)?
- [ ] Have I included all necessary information?
